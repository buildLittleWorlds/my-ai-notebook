Glossary & Key Concepts

Essential terms for AI hermeneutics. Each entry gives a crisp definition plus a why it matters for AI texts note so readers can immediately apply it in practice.

Absolute Distanciation (editor’s term)
Shorthand for the extreme textual distance of AI outputs: unlike human writing (which becomes autonomous through inscription), AI texts begin without an originating consciousness.

Why it matters: It justifies author-agnostic reading methods that focus on form, reception, and the text's projected world.

Appropriation
In Ricoeur's hermeneutics, the reader’s act of making the meaning of a text one’s own.

Why it matters: With no human intention to recover, well-argued appropriation becomes the ethical goal of interpretation.

Author-Function (Foucault)
The idea that the "author" is not a person but a social function that groups texts, confers authority, and establishes a mode of discourse.

Why it matters: Allows us to analyze how a model like GPT-4 functions *as an author* without having to claim it is a person.

Cyborg Text
Borrowing from Donna Haraway, a text co-produced by human and machine (e.g., via prompting and editing), requiring hybrid interpretive strategies.

Why it matters: Reminds us that much "AI writing" is a human-machine collaboration.

Death of the Author (Barthes)
Barthes’ thesis that meaning is generated in the act of reading a "tissue of quotations," not recovered from an author's mind.

Why it matters: AI texts, trained on vast corpora, are a literal "tissue of quotations," making them a prime object for post-authorial reading.

Distanciation (Ricoeur)
Ricoeur’s account of how writing separates discourse from its speaker and context, enabling semantic autonomy.

Why it matters: Provides the philosophical justification for evaluating AI texts by their structure and projected world, not by a missing psyche.

Fusion of Horizons (Gadamer)
The idea that understanding is a dialogue where the reader's worldview and the text's historical world merge, transforming the reader.

Why it matters: Frames the concept of being "read by a book" as a transformative event, providing a baseline for analyzing the new risks of being "read by an AI."

Grammatical Rail (Schleiermacher)
Interpretation via a text's relationship to the shared system of language and genre. For AI, this is the analysis of the model's architecture and training data.

Why it matters: A foundational part of any reading, but insufficient without its counterpart. Modern methods often replace the "psychological rail" with form and reception analysis.

Hermeneutics of Suspicion (Ricoeur)
An interpretive style (associated with Marx, Nietzsche, Freud) that unmasks hidden ideologies, biases, or power structures beneath a text's surface.

Why it matters: The essential stance for analyzing hidden biases in training data and the potential for AI texts to function as propaganda.

Horizon of Expectations (Jauss)
The set of cultural, ethical, and aesthetic assumptions a community of readers brings to a text at a given historical moment.

Why it matters: Allows us to measure the "aesthetic distance" of an AI text—does it merely conform to expectations, or does it challenge and change them?

Implied Reader (Iser)
A role or script embedded within a text that the actual reader must perform to make meaning. The text actively shapes its reader by creating "gaps" to be filled.

Why it matters: Provides a precise tool for analyzing how an AI text guides and constrains the user's interpretation, independent of any authorial intent.

Narratology (Genette)
The structural analysis of narrative, focusing on elements like order, duration, frequency, mood (focalization), and voice.

Why it matters: Offers a rigorous, objective method for analyzing the formal properties of AI-generated stories, bypassing the need for an author.

Paratext (Genette)
The material that surrounds a main text and shapes its interpretation, such as titles, prefaces, disclaimers, and even the user interface.

Why it matters: For AI, the prompt, system messages, and safety warnings are crucial paratexts that must be included in any rigorous interpretation.

Psychological Rail (Schleiermacher)
Reconstructing an author’s singular creative act. Inapplicable to AI in its original form, this interpretive goal is now often replaced by reception and form diagnostics.

Why it matters: Understanding its historical role helps clarify why author-agnostic methods like narratology are so crucial for AI.

Surveillance Capitalism (Zuboff)
An economic logic that treats human experience as free raw material for data extraction, analysis, and the creation of prediction products.

Why it matters: Provides the key framework for understanding the risks of "being read by an AI," where our interactions become data for a predictive system.

World of the Text (Ricoeur)
The possible world or mode of being that a text projects in front of itself, which the reader can inhabit through interpretation.

Why it matters: Offers an ethical criterion for judging an AI text: does it open up a livable, coherent, and valuable world?

Key Thinkers and Relevance

Roland Barthes (1915–1980)
His "Death of the Author" is the theoretical starting point for reading authorless texts.
Hans-Georg Gadamer (1900–2002)
His dialogical model of reading explains how texts can "read us" back.
Gérard Genette (1930–2018)
Provides the essential tools of narratology and paratextual analysis for a formal reading of AI texts.
Wolfgang Iser (1926–2007)
His concepts of the "implied reader" and "gaps" are central to analyzing user interaction with AI.
Hans Robert Jauss (1921–1997)
Reception theory allows us to study the meaning of AI texts as a social and historical phenomenon.
Paul Ricoeur (1913–2005)
His theories of distanciation and the "world of the text" provide a positive ethical program for reading AI.
