Glossary & Key Concepts

Essential terms for AI hermeneutics. Each entry gives a crisp definition plus a why it matters for AI texts note so readers can immediately apply it in practice.

Absolute Distanciation (editor’s term)
Shorthand for the extreme textual distance of AI outputs: unlike human writing (which becomes autonomous through inscription), AI texts begin without an originating consciousness.

Why it matters: It motivates method choices that read from the text and its system rather than behind it.

Algorithmic Authorship
Textual production by statistical sequence modeling rather than conscious intention; mimics many authorial functions without human agency.

Why it matters: Reframes authorship debates (credit, responsibility, style) as questions about systems, data, and prompts.

Appropriation
In Ricoeur, the reader’s making the text one’s own through understanding.

Why it matters: With no human intention to recover, well-argued appropriation becomes the ethical end of interpretation.

"As If" Framework
Treat AI outputs as if authored to unlock established tools, while remembering the stance is heuristic, not metaphysical.

Why it matters: Lets you use familiar author-centered questions without reifying a non-existent psyche.

Authorial Intention
The meaning an author aimed to convey; not available for AI outputs.

Why it matters: Pushes analysis toward textual form, system behavior, and reader uptake.

Composite / Distributed Authorship
AI outputs condense traces of many human texts via algorithmic mediation.

Why it matters: Supports source criticism, data ethics, and licensing questions.

Cyborg Text
Borrowing Haraway, a hybrid human–machine discourse requiring new interpretive strategies.

Why it matters: Reminds us that prompts, interfaces, and post-editing are human-machine co-productions.

Death of the Author
Barthes’ thesis that meaning belongs to language and readers, not (only) to authors.

Why it matters: AI texts literalize the claim, foregrounding reading communities over provenance.

Deconstruction
Derrida’s practice of reading binaries, hierarchies, and internal tensions.

Why it matters: Template-driven language often exposes its own seams; deconstruction shows where.

Distanciation
Ricoeur’s account of how writing separates discourse from speaker and context, enabling semantic autonomy.

Why it matters: Justifies evaluating AI texts by their worlds and structures, not by missing psyches.

Emergent / Statistical Meaning
Meaning that arises from patterned use rather than from a psyche’s design.

Why it matters: Explains why outputs can be illuminating even when no one “meant” them.

Gerede (Idle Talk)
Heidegger’s term for everyday, circulating discourse. Not purely pejorative; a constitutive mode of being-with-others.

Why it matters: Frames LLM prose as public talk that discloses a world while tending to averageness and repetition.

Grammatical Rail (Schleiermacher)
Interpretation via language, genre, and convention. In AI, this becomes analysis of model architecture, training signals, and genre mimicry.

Why it matters: Grounds “system-first” readings: what distributional and genre constraints shaped this output?

Hermeneutic Circle
Parts and whole co-illuminate. For AI, parts ↔ system patterns.

Why it matters: Encourages iterative hypothesize–test cycles across many generations.

Hermeneutics of Suspicion
Ricoeur’s label for Marx, Nietzsche, and Freud’s unmasking style of interpretation.

Why it matters: Useful for surfacing bias, ideology, and latent value-claims in training data and prompts.

Iterability
Derrida’s point that signs function by repeatability across contexts.

Why it matters: LLMs literalize iterability; moving a phrase between prompts is an interpretive probe.

Machine Hermeneutics
Interpretation adapted to non-human textual production, combining forensic, deconstructive, and pragmatic lenses.

Why it matters: Names the field this Kit is helping to build.

Organic Form
Coleridge’s idea of form growing from inner necessity (vs. mechanical template).

Why it matters: A diagnostic contrast for AI outputs that satisfy templates but lack developing necessity.

Pattern Psychology
A substitute for author-psychology: recurrent “tics,” compositional habits, and value defaults of a system.

Why it matters: Lets you speak of style without positing a mind.

Prompt Engineering
Designing inputs and scaffolds to steer outputs.

Why it matters: Operationalizes “functional intention” in the absence of an author’s.

Textual Objectivity (Betti)
Betti argued for objective meaning oriented by authorial intention and normative canons of interpretation. He did not coin “pseudo‑text,” but his framework raises doubts about whether intentionless outputs count as texts in a strict sense.

Why it matters: Clarifies where and why some traditions will resist treating AI outputs as texts proper.

Psychological Rail (Schleiermacher)
Reconstructing an author’s singular act. Inapplicable to AI; replace with system analysis and pattern psychology.

Why it matters: Keeps us from chasing a non-existent psyche.

Semantic Autonomy
Text’s independence from its maker achieved through writing.

Why it matters: Underwrites evaluating AI texts on the merits of what they say/do now.

Trace Logic
Meaning arises through traces of other texts and absences (Derrida).

Why it matters: Suggests training-data archaeology and intertext mapping as core practices.

World of the Text
Ricoeur’s name for the possible world projected by a text.

Why it matters: Offers a criterion of success: does the AI text open a livable, thinkable world?

Key Thinkers and Relevance

Roland Barthes (1915–1980)
Centres meaning in language/readers; see “The Death of the Author”.
Jacques Derrida (1930–2004)
Iterability and deconstruction show why meaning works without presence.
Hans‑Georg Gadamer (1900–2002)
Dialogue with tradition; AI texts as mediated conversations with culture.
Martin Heidegger (1889–1976)
Gerede (idle talk) and Gestell (enframing) illuminate AI’s style and milieu.
Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)
Perspectivism and suspicion sharpen critique of value‑claims in outputs.
Paul Ricoeur (1913–2005)
Distanciation, world‑of‑the‑text, and appropriation give a positive program for reading authorless texts.
Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834)
His grammatical/psychological dual method motivates our shifts to system and pattern analysis.
