53 Steelmanning the Romantics: A More Rigorous Toolkit for a Troubled Idea

For dozens of posts, I've been diagnosing what I call the "Romantic trace" in our contemporary debates about AI. I've argued that our anxieties about authenticity, the "human spark," and soulless machines are echoes of a worldview we inherited from the 1800s—a worldview I've suggested is inadequate for our current technological moment.

But a critique is only as good as its understanding of the thing it criticizes. Have I been fair? Or have I been attacking a caricature—a "Romanticism" of sentimental feelings and diary-like sincerity? By using the term as a shorthand for a set of gut feelings, I risk making the same mistake I'm accusing others of: relying on an inherited, unexamined concept.

This series of posts is an attempt to correct that. It is not a defense of Romanticism, nor is it an abandonment of my core critique. It's an exercise in intellectual honesty. Before we can definitively say that the Romantic worldview fails us, we must engage with its most powerful and rigorous form. We have to steelman the argument.

My new claim is this: Romanticism wasn't just a collection of feelings; it was a sophisticated intellectual movement that built a powerful method for understanding texts, consciousness, and meaning. To strengthen my own argument, I need to understand that method from the inside.

What This Series Will Do

This is not a conversion narrative. It is a strategic deep dive. The goal is to build a better toolkit for analysis by first understanding the tools of the very worldview I've been questioning. This series will do three things:

  • Isolate the Method: We will move past the caricature of "sincere feelings" and reconstruct the disciplined, evidence-based interpretive practices the Romantics actually developed. The "author," for them, was not a ghost to be channeled but a reconstructable practice to be traced in the text. We need to understand the logic of this method, even if we ultimately reject its premises.
  • Provide a Diagnostic Toolkit: I'm creating a Romantic Quick Kit to serve as the intellectual home base for this series. Think of it not as a user manual to be adopted, but as a field guide to a foreign country. It contains the key concepts and a step-by-step breakdown of the method. Use it to understand the internal logic of the system we are analyzing.
  • Stress-Test the Method Against AI: Each post in this series will use this toolkit to analyze the core problems of AI. The goal isn't to prove that the Romantic method works. The goal is to ask a more interesting question: What happens when you apply this powerful, 200-year-old interpretive engine to a 21st-century technology it could never have anticipated? What can it illuminate, and where do its foundational assumptions finally break down?

How to Read This Series

Start with the toolkit, not as a manual to be followed, but as a map of the territory we are about to explore. The posts that follow are experiments. They apply the Romantic method not to celebrate it, but to understand its mechanics, its power, and its limits.

This series is an argument that to move past the Romantic worldview that still quietly shapes so much of our thinking, we first have to understand it, in its own terms, better than it understood itself.

This post inaugurates the "Steelmanning the Romantics" series. The Quick Kit is the essential starting point for the posts that follow.
