57 The Interpreter’s Blind Spot: Why Analyzing the System Isn't Enough

Romantic Hermeneutics — Quick Kit

Analysis of AI-generated text often defaults to a systematic view: we talk about the model's architecture, the biases in its training data, the statistical probabilities of its word choices. This is necessary work. But if it's all we do, we commit a fundamental interpretive error. We are trying to understand a text by looking at only one half of the evidence.

Romantic hermeneutics, as laid out by Schleiermacher, insists that true understanding requires a double vision. It demands that we operate on two rails at once: the grammatical/systematic and the psychological/singular.

The Lure of the System

In AI discourse, the grammatical rail—the analysis of the system—is dominant. We can produce neat visualizations, map the dataset, and call it "understanding." But this approach, on its own, is a form of reductionism. It treats the text as a mere symptom of the system that produced it.

Romantic method insists that we must also engage the psychological rail: the analysis of the work as a singular artifact, a unique constellation of choices that could have been otherwise. This means asking *how* this specific output, with its unique metaphors and stylistic tics, was composed.

What's Lost When One Rail Disappears

When we ignore the psychological rail, interpretation collapses into something else entirely. We lose three crucial capacities:

  • From Interpretation to Taxonomy: We stop interpreting the work and start classifying it. The text becomes just another data point to be sorted, an example of the system's behavior. We can describe what it is, but we can't account for how it achieves its specific effects.
  • Blindness to Emergent Meaning: A system-only view can never account for how works say more than the system "knows." It misses the accidental genius, the productive contradictions, and the surplus meaning that emerges from the unique combination of elements in a specific text.
  • The End of Dialogue: Without positing a singular "voice" or practice to analyze (even a synthetic one), the interpreter has nothing to engage with. We are left measuring an environment, not entering into a dialogue with an artifact.

Why This Matters

A purely systematic analysis can explain the general probabilities that made a text possible. But it can never explain the specific choices that make a text actual. Recovering the other half—the psychological rail—is what allows us to move from explaining a process to understanding a work. It is what makes our work hermeneutics, not just data science.
