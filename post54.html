54 The Author-Function in the Machine: A Romantic Test

The most common critique of AI writing is that there is "no one there." This assumes the Romantic ideal of an "author" as a soul pouring its meaning into the text. But the rigorous Romantics started from a more skeptical place: Schleiermacher famously argued that misunderstanding is the norm, and therefore interpretation must be a disciplined art. A core part of that art was reconstructing the author's singular practice.

This wasn't just empathy; it was a formal method. It involved both a comparative analysis (tracing patterns against the rules of language) and a "divinatory" one—making a disciplined, intuitive leap to hypothesize the creative act that could produce such a text. So, let's run this more complex test. Can we apply it to an AI?

A Practical Demonstration

I prompted an AI: "Write a four-line poem about a city at night."

The velvet dark doth cloak the stone,
Where steel and glass in slumber lie,
A million lights, like seeds are sown,
Beneath a silent, watching sky.

Applying the method:

1. The Grammatical/Comparative Rail

The poem uses shared conventions: quatrain, iambic-ish meter, ABCB rhyme, familiar tropes. This is the baseline evidence.

2. The Psychological/Divinatory Rail

Now we make our leap. We see the archaic "doth" and the tension between the organic ("seeds") and artificial ("steel"). Our divinatory guess is that the organizing "mind" here has a signature: it favors a slightly elevated, historical tone and is preoccupied with the nature/tech divide. We could then test this hypothesis against its other outputs.

The Critical Pivot: A Discovery or a Projection?

The method works. We successfully generated a plausible author-function for the machine, complete with a stylistic signature. But what have we actually discovered?

This is where the steelman exercise reveals its true purpose. The Romantic method, with its powerful two-stroke engine of analysis and divination, is so effective at constructing a coherent author that it can build one even from a statistical ghost. It proves not that the AI has a soul, but that our interpretive methods are hardwired to find one. We have successfully projected the ghost into the machine.

The question, then, is not whether an AI can have a "voice." The more unsettling question is whether the "voice" we've always found in human texts is also, in part, a similar reconstruction—a coherent pattern our minds assemble after the fact. Applying this more rigorous Romantic method to AI doesn't elevate the machine; it forces us to question the nature of the very "presence" we thought was so simple to begin with.
