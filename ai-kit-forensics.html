AI Text Forensics

How to read machine-written text like a forensic linguist.

What this is: A practical guide to identifying the likely presence of AI in a text and to reading it well if it is.

What this is not: A magical detector. There is no single test that proves a text was written by AI. This is a method for weighing evidence.

Why Forensics?

Interpretation starts with the artifact. Think of this like Lorenzo Valla analyzing the Donation of Constantine: he didn’t ask what people believed; he examined the Latin. You’re doing the same, studying style, syntax, structure, and sources.

The Four Forensic Dimensions

1. Grammatical Analysis: The System's Fingerprints

Every AI model has characteristic patterns that function like linguistic fingerprints.

What to Look For:

  • Formulaic Transitions: "Furthermore,"...In conclusion," transitional phrases that appear with unnatural frequency
  • Hedge Language: "It seems," "appears to be," "might suggest." Statistical uncertainty made linguistic
  • List Structures: Artificial preference for numbered lists and bullet points
  • Semantic Drift: Gradual topic changes that follow associational rather than logical patterns
  • Register Inconsistency: Mixing formal academic language with casual expressions

Methods:

  • Look for repetition patterns in phrasing, especially near paragraph boundaries
  • Scan for “thesaurus spirals”: rare synonyms used inconsistently
  • Check punctuation habits: em dashes vs commas vs semicolons

2. Training Data Archaeology: Tracing the Sources

AI texts reflect statistical patterns from their training data (and may sometimes memorize or regurgitate snippets). Like archaeological layers, you can sometimes identify the strata.

What to Look For:

  • Temporal Anachronism: Sources implicitly from different eras mashed together
  • Genre Blending: Academic diction with pop journalism cadence
  • Canonical Gravity: Over-reliance on “obvious” examples and quotations
  • Surface Paraphrase: Rephrasings that preserve order and emphasis of a likely source

Methods:

  • Extract distinctive phrases (5–8 words) and search for near-matches
  • Map references: which authors/titles recur, and how are they combined?
  • Compare metaphors: do images feel generic or oddly mixed?

3. Logical Profile: How the Text Moves

AI writing often follows probabilistic transitions rather than argumentative necessity.

What to Look For:

  • Centerless Summaries: Everything is relevant; nothing is decisive
  • Over-symmetric Structure: 3–5 tidy sections with parallel subpoints regardless of topic
  • Confidence + Vagueness: Authoritative tone with low-commitment claims

Methods:

  • Outline the argument—does each step necessitate the next, or just plausibly follow?
  • Check citations: are they integral to claims or ornament on the surface?
  • Test for “rubber logic”: replace key terms—does the paragraph still read plausibly?

4. Signature Artifacts: Known Model Behaviors

Some behaviors are widely documented in contemporary LLMs.

What to Look For:

  • Repetition/Bluntness: looping phrases, bland generalities
  • Over-structure: boilerplate intros/outros; stock “takeaways” sections
  • Context Loss: difficulty retrieving mid-document details in long contexts

Methods:

  • Probe with specific follow-ups (“where did X come from?”). Look for confident mistakes
  • Move key details to the middle of a long prompt to test retrieval degradation
  • Ask for a rephrase in a constrained style to expose training-pattern defaults

Worked Example

Consider this AI-generated sonnet line: "The autumn leaves doth whisper secrets old." Forensic analysis reveals:

  • Grammatical: Archaic "doth" with modern "whisper"—a blend many contemporary poets would avoid unless intentionally anachronistic
  • Archaeological: Combines Shakespearean sonnet patterns with Romantic nature imagery
  • Logical: Follows statistical patterns... of "poetic" language without deeper metaphorical coherence

Decision Framework: Weighing Evidence

  1. Collect signals across the four dimensions
  2. Assign weights based on strength and independence of each signal
  3. Form a judgment about likelihood of AI authorship—not a proof, a probability
  4. Adapt your reading accordingly (see below)

How to Read AI Texts Better

  • Interrogate claims—require concrete references, not just authority tone
  • Track metaphors—note when imagery is generic or mixed
  • Force commitments—ask for choices and tradeoffs, not just summaries
  • Re-anchor—tie assertions to sources and inspect those sources
