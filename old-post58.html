58 “Better Than He Understood Himself”: Why Interpretation Is Irreducible

Romantic Hermeneutics — Quick Kit

One of Schleiermacher’s most audacious claims is that the goal of interpretation is to understand an author "better than he understood himself." To a computationalist, this sounds like nonsense. If meaning is a product of a generative process, the best one could ever do is perfectly replicate that process. How could an interpreter possibly surpass the source?

This question reveals the fundamental difference between two models of understanding. The computational model sees understanding as replication. The Romantic hermeneutic model sees it as illumination. This is why interpretation remains an irreducible human art.

The Computational Goal: To Replicate the Process

In much of the discourse around AI, the ultimate form of "understanding" a model is to successfully reverse-engineer it. If we can map its weights and trace its pathways to know *how* it produced an output, we consider the job done. Meaning is reduced to its generative mechanics. This is a powerful but limited view.

The Hermeneutic Goal: To Illuminate the Work

Romantic hermeneutics is not trying to replicate the author's brain state or the model's processing path. It is a productive act that adds layers of understanding to the work itself. To understand a work "better than" its creator means to:

  • Articulate the Tacit: The interpreter makes explicit the implicit rules, patterns, and stylistic habits that the author (or model) followed without full self-awareness. This is the work of the psychological rail.
  • Expand the Context: The interpreter places the work into historical, philosophical, and intertextual conversations that were unavailable to its creator, revealing new dimensions of its significance.
  • Reveal the Surplus: The interpreter uncovers the unconscious meanings and cultural logics that the work reveals in spite of itself, fulfilling Schlegel's insight that a work "aims at more than it knows."

An AI cannot do this, because this is not a generative task. It is an analytical and synthetic one. Interpretation doesn't just reproduce what's there; it creates a new, richer understanding of what the work *is* and *does* in the world.

Conclusion to the Series

This series, Defense of Romanticism, began as a response to the caricature of Romanticism as a naïve or sentimental worldview. By building the Quick Kit and applying its methods, we have shown that Romanticism is, in fact, a rigorous and profoundly relevant analytical framework for our technological moment.

It gives us the tools to move beyond simplistic binaries—human vs. machine, soul vs. code, sincerity vs. bullshit—and perform a more nuanced, evidence-based analysis of any text. It proves that the act of interpretation is not a nostalgic search for a ghost in the machine, but a vital, ongoing human project of making meaning. The work continues.
