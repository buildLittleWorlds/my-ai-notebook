55 More Than Bullshit? Reading the Textual Unconscious of an Unconscious Text

A powerful critique labels LLMs as "bullshit machines," indifferent to the truth. From a Romantic view, this is a charge of radical inauthenticity. The machine speaks words it does not inhabit.

But the Romantic toolkit was more sophisticated than this. Friedrich Schlegel argued that a great work "aims at more than it knows," carrying unconscious meanings. He even suggested that a text's "confusions" and contradictions were themselves meaningful, revealing the author's internal struggles. This is a method that embraces flaws. What happens if we apply this generous lens to AI?

From Bullshit to Artifact: An Example

Let's use our previous example: an AI prompted for a corporate statement on "innovation" produces text blending Silicon Valley jargon with agrarian metaphors ("planting the seeds of synergy," "harvesting disruptive ideas").

  • The "bullshit" reading dismisses this as a meaningless, contradictory cliché.
  • A Schlegelian hermeneutic reading, however, sees a rich artifact. The unintentional fusion—the "productive confusion"—isn't the AI's "idea." It's an unmediated expression of a deep-seated American myth (the Jeffersonian farmer reborn as the tech entrepreneur) that is latent in the training data. The AI, having no conscious intent to smooth over the contradiction, reveals it raw.

The Critical Pivot: Whose Unconscious Is It Anyway?

Here again, the rigorous method works, but reveals something deeply unsettling. We have successfully extracted a "surplus of meaning" from the text's "confusion." But is this meaning in the text, or is it entirely in us, the interpreters?

The AI knows nothing of Jefferson or Silicon Valley. It is just generating a probable sequence. We are the ones who recognize the pattern and dignify its contradiction as "productive." Schlegel’s method was designed to uncover the hidden depths of a human author's mind and time. Applied to AI, it reveals that the "textual unconscious" is our own. We are finding meaning not because the text is deep, but because our capacity for interpretation is.

This reframes the "bullshit" critique. Perhaps the true bullshit isn't the AI's indifference to truth, but the interpretive hubris of treating its statistical noise as a profound, symbolic dream. We ask the machine to dream for us, and then congratulate ourselves on our brilliant interpretation of the result.
