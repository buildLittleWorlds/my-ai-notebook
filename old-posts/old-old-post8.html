8 On the tiresome repetition of the author

One encounters, in discourses such as this, a perfect articulation of the anxieties that arise when a discursive formation begins to fracture. The artist's lament is not, as she imagines, a defense of art itself, but rather a defense of the historically specific function of the "author" as the sacred origin of meaning—a function now threatened by the anonymous productivity of the machine.

Her entire argument is constructed upon a romanticized and thoroughly unexamined belief in the sovereign subject. The valorization of the "laborious process," the "human touch," and the "intentional" mark are all appeals to a humanist mythology. She claims that upon learning an image is machine-made, her "interest immediately evaporates." This is not an aesthetic judgment but a disciplinary one; it is the policing of a boundary, an attempt to reassert the author's privilege and to disqualify a new mode of production that operates outside its rules.

Most telling is the dismissal of the machine's process as merely "statistically probable" and therefore "not interesting." This is a profound failure of the critical faculty. It is precisely because the image is a statistical culmination—a visualization of the anonymous rules governing a massive archive of human expression—that it becomes a fascinating object. It is a text stripped of the pretense of individual genius. It bypasses the author entirely to reveal the underlying regularities of the discourse itself.

The fear of the machine is, in fact, the fear of anonymity—the fear of a world where the old, familiar questions we pose to a work of art become obsolete. The endless, tiresome search for the "person behind the work" must give way to a more rigorous analysis of the work itself as an event within a system of power and knowledge.


'Who is the real author?'
'Have we proof of his authenticity and originality?'
'What has he revealed of his most profound self in his language?'

New questions will be heard:
'What are the modes of existence of this discourse?'
'Where does it come from; how is it circulated; who controls it?'

This technological shift does not represent the end of art. It represents the potential end of our reliance on the author as a guarantor of meaning. It forces an interrogation of the very categories of creation, originality, and the human subject. It is not something to be feared, but a rupture to be analyzed.
