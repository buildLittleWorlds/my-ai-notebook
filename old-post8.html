8 A beautiful and terrifying idea

The thing I keep noticing in the Barenholtz videos is his tone. He talks about some pretty unsettling ideas, but he's clearly energized by them. It's not the usual doom and gloom. It’s the excitement of a scientist who feels he's stumbled onto something fundamental.

"...what to me in some ways is even more interesting is that we're living through an inflection point in scientific and even philosophical history... we've captured something that is so profoundly important to human beings which is the nature of language."

— Dr. Elan Barenholtz, The Auto-Regression Theory of Cognition (17:01 in video)

You can hear the "wow" factor in his voice when he talks about how well these models work. It's not just an engineering success for him; it's a revelation:

"This thing is just ridiculously good. And so that just blows my mind!"

— Dr. Elan Barenholtz, The Theory That Shatters Language Itself (1:44:48 in video)

But the excitement is tied directly to how strange the implications are. He's not celebrating a tool that serves us, but a force that has its own life:

"...language is being downloaded into them. And now we know... it is this kind of its own self-running system. It's a product of society, not of any individual... And so there is this thing that's in us but not really of us that is running and it's driving most of our behavior."

— Dr. Elan Barenholtz, The (Terrifying) Theory That Your Thoughts Were Never Your Own (39:46 in video)

Comment: His excitement doesn't seem to come from a belief that AI will solve all our problems. It's the thrill of discovery. He sounds like a physicist who has just found a new, powerful, and slightly dangerous law of nature. He's not necessarily happy about all its implications, but he's undeniably in awe of the thing itself.

The terror and the beauty are connected. The discovery is exhilarating *because* it "shook and rocked" his world. It's the sublime thrill of realizing that the most human thing about us—our language—might not be ours at all.
